Research

My research explores how perception emerges from the dynamic and experience-dependent construction of sensory representations in cortex.

The constructive nature of perception

I am fascinated by the constructive nature of perception: transforming imprecise and discrete sensory inputs into a continuous, meaningful experience. Following R. L. Gregory, I view perception as hypothesis generation, guided by knowledge of the world’s regularities—from basic perceptual organization to higher-level expectations about objects and their properties.

Spatiotemporal context reshaping

If perception is hypothesis generation, contextual structure must shape the representation itself. In somatosensory cortex, temporally and spatially related tactile inputs can become cortically merged, forming unified representations rather than remaining independent signals—providing a substrate for tactile grouping.

Experience-driven reshaping

If cortical merging reflects basic regularities of co-occurrence, learning embeds task-specific knowledge into sensory codes. In mouse V1, training for orientation discrimination induces a systematic distortion of orientation representation, structuring population activity along behaviorally relevant dimensions and improving separability for perceptual judgments.

Geometry & perceptual decisions

I study sensory representations through the lens of high-dimensional geometry—manifold structure, separability, dimensionality, and task-aligned axes—to link population structure to discrimination and generalization. Ultimately, I aim to identify where behaviorally predictive population variables are read out and how downstream circuits transform these structured representations into categorical percepts and choices.
